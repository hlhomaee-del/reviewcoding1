🔑 Key Insight: In medical billing, diagnosis codes (ICD-10-CM) justify the service. Procedure codes (CPT or HCPCS) describe what was done. They must work together to show medical necessity!
🔵 Concept Check 1 of 5
A patient is seen for a laceration repair of the left hand. The provider uses a specific code to represent the repair procedure. What code set does this belong to?
A ICD-10-CM — it describes the injury (laceration)
B CPT — it represents the procedure performed by the physician
C HCPCS Level II — it represents the bandage supply used
D ICD-10-PCS — it represents the inpatient procedure
💡 Rationale
The laceration repair is a procedure performed by the physician. CPT codes represent physician/professional services and procedures — so the repair code would come from CPT. ICD-10-CM would capture the diagnosis (laceration of left hand). HCPCS would capture supplies (like bandages). ICD-10-PCS is only for hospital inpatient procedures, which this is not.
🔵 Concept Check 2 of 5
A hospitalized patient receives a surgical appendectomy performed in the hospital's operating room. The hospital coder needs to capture the procedure. Which code set would the hospital coder use for the procedure?
A CPT — it represents all surgical procedures
B ICD-10-CM — it describes the appendix condition
C HCPCS Level II — it covers hospital supplies
D ICD-10-PCS — it is used for hospital inpatient procedures
💡 Rationale
ICD-10-PCS (Procedure Coding System) is used exclusively by hospital facility coders for inpatient procedures. CPT is used for physician/professional billing, not hospital inpatient procedures. ICD-10-CM captures diagnoses. HCPCS covers supplies and services not in CPT.
🔍 Deeper Look: Each Code Set
ICD-10-CM
International Classification of Diseases, 10th Revision, Clinical Modification

Purpose: Documents diagnoses, conditions, signs, symptoms, and reasons for visits.
Used by: All healthcare settings (outpatient, inpatient, physician, hospital).
Why it matters: The diagnosis code supports medical necessity — it tells the payer WHY the patient needed the service.
Example: E11.9 = Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus without complications

CPT
Current Procedural Terminology

Purpose: Documents services, procedures, and evaluations by physicians and professionals.
Used by: Physician offices, clinics, outpatient facilities — professional billing.
Why it matters: CPT tells payers what was done — the service performed.
Example: 99213 = Office Visit, Established Patient, Level 3

HCPCS II
Healthcare Common Procedure Coding System Level II

Purpose: Covers supplies, durable medical equipment (DME), ambulance transport, injections, and other services NOT found in CPT.
Used by: All healthcare settings, especially for billing Medicare and Medicaid.
Example: A6216 = Gauze, non-impregnated, non-sterile; A0428 = Ambulance service, basic life support

ICD-10-PCS
Procedure Coding System

Purpose: Documents procedures performed on hospital inpatients by the facility.
Used by: Hospital coders for inpatient facility billing ONLY — NOT for physician billing.
Why it matters: Completely different from CPT — ICD-10-PCS uses 7-character alphanumeric codes.
Example: 0DTJ4ZZ = Inspection of Large Intestine, Percutaneous Endoscopic Approach

🔵 Concept Check 3 of 5
A home health agency bills for a wheelchair provided to a patient. Which code set would most likely be used for the wheelchair?
A ICD-10-CM — it describes the patient's mobility diagnosis
B CPT — it covers all durable medical equipment
C HCPCS Level II — it covers supplies and equipment like wheelchairs
D ICD-10-PCS — it is used for all durable equipment in hospitals
💡 Rationale
HCPCS Level II covers durable medical equipment (DME), supplies, and services not found in CPT. A wheelchair is durable medical equipment, so it is billed using HCPCS Level II codes. ICD-10-CM would capture the diagnosis that necessitates the wheelchair. CPT does not cover DME.
🔵 Concept Check 4 of 5
Which code set is specifically designed to support MEDICAL NECESSITY — explaining to the payer why the patient required the service?
A ICD-10-CM — diagnosis codes justify the medical need
B CPT — procedure codes explain medical necessity
C HCPCS Level II — supply codes establish necessity
D ICD-10-PCS — inpatient codes establish necessity
💡 Rationale
ICD-10-CM diagnosis codes support medical necessity. They tell the payer WHY the patient needed the service. Without a supporting diagnosis code, the procedure code (CPT or HCPCS) may be denied as not medically necessary.
🔵 Concept Check 5 of 5
SCENARIO: A claim is submitted with only a CPT code for a knee injection. There is no diagnosis code. What is the most likely outcome?
A The claim will be paid immediately — CPT is enough
B The claim will likely be denied — there is no diagnosis to support medical necessity
C The payer will automatically assign a diagnosis code
D The claim will be sent to ICD-10-PCS review
💡 Rationale
A claim submitted without a diagnosis code lacks medical necessity support. Payers require ICD-10-CM diagnosis codes to justify that the service was medically necessary. A claim with only a CPT code and no diagnosis will typically be denied.

📋 SOAP Note: What Each Section Means
S — Subjective
What the patient SAYS
Chief complaint, history, pain description, reported symptoms
"I have a headache"
O — Objective
What the provider MEASURES or OBSERVES
Vitals, physical exam findings, lab results, test results
"BP: 145/90"
A — Assessment
Provider's DIAGNOSIS or clinical impression
This is where the CODEABLE DIAGNOSIS lives!
"Hypertension, uncontrolled"
P — Plan
What will happen NEXT
Medications, referrals, follow-up, procedures ordered
"Start lisinopril 10mg daily"
🔑 Coding Key: Coders primarily look at the Assessment (A) section for confirmed diagnoses. The Subjective section may list symptoms, but if a confirmed diagnosis is documented in the Assessment, the confirmed diagnosis is coded — not the symptoms (when symptoms are integral to the diagnosis).

📋 SOAP Coding — Question 1
Using the SOAP note above (costochondritis), which section would a coder look at to find the CODEABLE DIAGNOSIS?
A S — Subjective: the patient reported chest pain
B O — Objective: the vital signs show high blood pressure
C A — Assessment: "Costochondritis, acute" is the confirmed diagnosis
D P — Plan: the medication prescribed indicates the diagnosis
💡 Rationale
The Assessment section is where the provider documents their clinical impression — the confirmed diagnosis. Coders primarily look at the Assessment (A) for the codeable diagnosis. The Subjective and Objective sections provide supporting information, but the Assessment is where the confirmed diagnosis lives.
📋 SOAP Coding — Question 2
In the costochondritis SOAP note, should a coder separately code the patient's chest pain (reported in the Subjective section)?
A No — chest pain is a symptom integral to costochondritis; the confirmed diagnosis is coded
B Yes — all symptoms the patient mentions must be coded separately
C Yes — chest pain always gets its own ICD-10-CM code
D No — symptoms are never coded in any situation
💡 Rationale
When a confirmed diagnosis has been documented, coders code the diagnosis — not the symptoms that are integral to (part of) that diagnosis. Chest pain is a classic symptom of costochondritis. Since costochondritis is the confirmed Assessment diagnosis, the chest pain is not separately coded. (Note: symptoms CAN be coded separately when they are NOT integral to the diagnosis, or when no confirmed diagnosis is documented.)

📋 SOAP Practice — Q1
For Mr. Thompson's note, what is the codeable diagnosis?
A Fatigue, increased thirst, and frequent urination — these are the presenting symptoms
B Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus, uncontrolled — this is the confirmed Assessment diagnosis
C HbA1c: 9.2% — this is the lab result driving the code
D Metformin prescription — medications always indicate the primary diagnosis code
💡 Rationale
The Assessment documents "Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus, uncontrolled" — this is the confirmed diagnosis and is what gets coded with ICD-10-CM. The symptoms in the Subjective (fatigue, thirst, frequent urination) are integral to uncontrolled diabetes and are not separately coded when this confirmed diagnosis is present.
📋 SOAP Practice — Q2
Should the coder separately code Mr. Thompson's fatigue, increased thirst, and frequent urination?
A No — these symptoms are integral to Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus, which is the confirmed diagnosis
B Yes — all subjective complaints must always be coded
C Yes — they are documented in the SOAP note, so they must be coded
D Only fatigue should be coded separately
💡 Rationale
Fatigue, increased thirst (polydipsia), and frequent urination (polyuria) are classic signs and symptoms of diabetes mellitus. Since the confirmed diagnosis of Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus has been documented, these integral symptoms are NOT separately coded. The confirmed diagnosis code already captures the clinical picture.
📋 SOAP Practice — Q3 (No Diagnosis Scenario)
SCENARIO: A patient presents with chest pain and shortness of breath. After workup, the physician documents: "Assessment: Chest pain and dyspnea — etiology unclear, to be investigated." NO confirmed diagnosis is given. What should the coder do?
A Code acute myocardial infarction — the most likely cause of these symptoms
B Do not code anything — wait for a confirmed diagnosis
C Code the documented signs and symptoms (chest pain + dyspnea) since there is no confirmed diagnosis
D Code "rule out" as the primary diagnosis
💡 Rationale
When no confirmed diagnosis has been documented, coders code the signs and symptoms that are documented. Coders do NOT speculate or code a diagnosis that has only been suspected or "ruled out." In outpatient coding, symptoms are coded when no definitive diagnosis has been established. In this case: code chest pain AND dyspnea separately, as both are documented.

✅ Confirmed Diagnosis
Rule: When a provider documents a confirmed, definitive diagnosis in the Assessment, CODE THE DIAGNOSIS — not the individual symptoms. Symptoms that are integral (part of) the confirmed diagnosis are not separately coded.
✅ CODE THIS
Assessment: Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus
→ Code: E11.9 (T2DM without complications)
🚫 DO NOT SEPARATELY CODE
Subjective: "I feel tired and thirsty all the time"
→ These are integral symptoms of T2DM
🟢 Confirmed Dx — Q1
Assessment: "Essential hypertension." The patient also reported headaches in the Subjective. Should the headache be coded separately?
A Generally no — headache can be a symptom of hypertension; if it is integral and not separately treated, it is not coded
B Yes — always code every symptom the patient reports
C Yes — headache has its own ICD-10-CM code so it must be coded
D No — symptoms are never coded under any circumstances
💡 Rationale
When a confirmed diagnosis is present, symptoms integral to that diagnosis are generally not separately coded. Headache can be associated with hypertension. If the headache is not separately evaluated, treated, or documented as a distinct condition, it is not separately coded. The confirmed diagnosis (essential hypertension) is the code.
⚠️ When to Code Symptoms
Rule: Code signs and symptoms when NO confirmed diagnosis has been established. Do not speculate or assume a diagnosis. Code only what is documented.
⚠️ SYMPTOM CODING SITUATION
Assessment: "Nausea and vomiting — cause undetermined."
→ Code nausea AND vomiting separately
→ Do NOT assume gastroenteritis or another diagnosis
✅ KEY RULE
In outpatient settings, "rule out," "possible," "suspected," or "probable" diagnoses are NOT coded as if confirmed.
→ Code the symptoms instead
⚠️ Symptom Coding — Q1
Assessment: "Abdominal pain — possible appendicitis, to be ruled out." What should be coded in an outpatient setting?
A Appendicitis — the physician suspects it
B Abdominal pain — no confirmed diagnosis; code the documented symptom
C Rule-out appendicitis — use the "rule out" code
D Leave it blank — wait until the diagnosis is confirmed
💡 Rationale
In outpatient settings, "possible," "probable," "suspected," and "rule out" diagnoses are NOT coded as if confirmed. Only code what IS confirmed. Since only abdominal pain has been confirmed in the documentation, that is what is coded. There is no ICD-10-CM "rule out" code — this is a concept only.
⚠️ Symptom Coding — Q2
A patient presents with dizziness and nausea. After examination, the provider's Assessment states: "Vertigo, NOS." Should dizziness and nausea be coded separately?
A No — dizziness and nausea are typically integral symptoms of vertigo; the confirmed diagnosis is coded
B Yes — both must be coded since they were the patient's complaints
C Only nausea — dizziness is part of vertigo but nausea is separate
D Only code if the provider documents them in the Assessment
💡 Rationale
Dizziness and nausea are classic signs integral to vertigo. When a confirmed diagnosis of vertigo is documented in the Assessment, the integral symptoms (dizziness, nausea) are not separately coded. The confirmed diagnosis of Vertigo, NOS would be coded with ICD-10-CM.

🔗 Dx + Service — Q1
A coder submits a claim with CPT 93000 (ECG) and ICD-10-CM R07.9 (chest pain). Why is this pairing important for medical necessity?
A The chest pain diagnosis (ICD-10-CM) justifies WHY the ECG was ordered — showing it was medically necessary
B The ECG code (CPT) tells the payer WHY the patient had chest pain
C The pairing is not important — either code alone is sufficient
D The CPT code establishes the diagnosis for billing purposes
💡 Rationale
The ICD-10-CM code (chest pain) supports medical necessity by explaining WHY the ECG was needed. The CPT code (ECG) tells the payer WHAT was done. Payers require this pairing to confirm the service was appropriate for the patient's condition. Without a supporting diagnosis, the claim may be denied.
🔗 Dx + Service — Q2 (Identify the Role)
A claim shows: ICD-10-CM: J06.9 (Acute upper respiratory infection) + CPT: 99213 (Office Visit). Which code explains WHY the patient was seen, and which explains WHAT was done?
A J06.9 explains WHY (diagnosis = medical necessity); 99213 explains WHAT (office visit service was performed)
B 99213 explains WHY; J06.9 explains WHAT
C Both codes explain WHY — CPT also includes diagnosis information
D Both codes explain WHAT — the visit and the diagnosis are the same thing
💡 Rationale
ICD-10-CM diagnosis codes (like J06.9) answer WHY — they document the condition that required care and establish medical necessity. CPT codes (like 99213) answer WHAT — they document the service or procedure performed. These two code sets work together on every claim.
